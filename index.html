Hierarchy in Gilead

Commanders

Highest-ranking men. They control politics, households, and the laws of Gilead.

Guardians / Eyes

Men who serve as soldiers, guards, or secret police.

Wives

Married to Commanders. They have status, but little real independence.

Aunts

Women who train and control the Handmaids. They enforce Gilead's ideology.

Handmaids

Fertile women forced to bear children for powerful families.

Marthas

Domestic servants responsible for cooking, cleaning, and household work.

Unwomen

Women rejected by Gilead and sent away to the Colonies.

Why Is It Dystopian?

01

A near-collapse of human reproduction has created a world where healthy births are extremely rare. With only about one healthy birth in five and birth rates reaching a new low, the crisis becomes the foundation for Gilead's dystopian order.

02

Gilead is ruled by a totalitarian government that oppresses women and treats them as objects. Women are not allowed to read or own money, and the government uses the birth-rate crisis to justify its control and claim moral righteousness.

03

Not following the system can mean death. People who disagree with Gilead or break its rules may be hanged on the Wall, turning fear and public punishment into tools of control.

Meaning in The Handmaid's Tale

The phrase "Nolite te bastardes carborundorum" is a secret message left by the previous Handmaid in Offred's room.

Although it is mock Latin and does not have a real meaning, it can be understood as "Don't let the bastards grind you down."

For Offred, the phrase becomes a symbol of hope and resistance. It reminds her that others have suffered before her and encourages her to keep her identity and dignity despite the oppression of Gilead.

The quote shows that even in a system designed to control people, small acts of resistance can help individuals stay strong and survive.

Meaning in Today's World

Today, the quote can be seen as a message of resilience and self-respect.

It encourages people not to give up when facing unfair treatment, discrimination, or pressure from society. The phrase reminds us to stand up for our rights and beliefs, even when situations seem difficult.

For example, people who face bullying, inequality, or political oppression can see the quote as a reminder to stay strong and not let others take away their confidence or freedom.

It highlights the importance of courage and perseverance in challenging times.

Meaning in The Handmaid's Tale

The quote shows how people allowed Gilead to rise by deliberately avoiding the truth. It wasn’t that they didn’t know what was happening, they chose not to confront the warning signs because doing so felt uncomfortable or risky. This active avoidance becomes a form of complicity, revealing how dangerous it is when a society convinces itself that looking away is easier than resisting. The line exposes the quiet, everyday choices that help oppressive systems grow long before they become impossible to stop.

Ignoring isn't the same as ignorance

Meaning in Today's World

The quote highlights how ignoring problems like injustice, misinformation, or threats to democracy is rarely accidental. It takes effort to avoid facts, to silence doubts, or to pretend that uncomfortable realities don’t exist. Choosing comfort over awareness can allow harmful structures to strengthen unnoticed. The message urges us to stay alert and responsible, reminding us that denial is an active choice and one that shapes the world just as much as action does.

Comment by Emma

Meaning in The Handmaid's Tale

The Commander uses this phrase to justify the creation of Gilead. In his view, "better" means solving the fertility crisis through forced surrogacy, reducing pollution, and establishing a strict social order. But "worse for some" reveals the cruelty behind that order: only certain people are granted safety, power, and rights.

The quote shows that the Commander understands that June's life as Offred is unbearable, but he treats her suffering as the cost of achieving what he sees as a greater social good.

Better never means better for everyone
Comment by Emma

Meaning in Today's World

Atwood uses this quote as a warning about problems that still exist today. It shows how political, technological, and economic progress is often distributed unevenly.

For example, AI and new technologies can increase efficiency and corporate profit, while some workers face more pressure, fewer protections, and less control over their labor. The quote warns us not to accept progress blindly, but to ask who benefits, who pays the price, and whose rights are being sacrificed.
